## What Is Episodic Memory?

Episodic memory is defined as the ability to anchor memories of personal events to their spatiotemporal context — the “what-where-when” (W-W-W) framework. Unlike simple item memory (e.g., recognizing a face), true episodic memory requires binding *what* happened to *where* and *when* it occurred. Tulving further proposed that authentic episodic retrieval involves *autonoesis* — the conscious experience of mentally re-living a past event. [Bevandic et al., 2024](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11129319/)

## The Developmental Timeline: A Staged Emergence

Episodic memory does not arrive as a single capacity; it builds progressively across distinct components.

### Stage 1: Object (“What”) Encoding — Earliest to Emerge

The most basic building block — simply recognizing an object as familiar or novel — develops first and earliest across all species studied. In humans, novelty preferences on the Visual Paired Comparison (VPC) task appear even in neonates. In non-human primates (NHPs), mature object encoding is present by ~1.5 months of age (equivalent to ~6 months in humans), and in rodents it emerges reliably by postnatal day 14 (P14). This early competency in “what” memory is notably independent of the hippocampus early in life, being supported instead by the perirhinal and parahippocampal cortices. [Bevandic et al., 2024](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11129319/)

### Stage 2: What-Where (Contextual and Spatial) Encoding — Inflection at ~2 Years

The ability to bind objects to their spatial location or environmental context develops significantly later. In humans: [Bevandic et al., 2024](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11129319/)

-   At **12 months**, infants cannot flexibly separate an object from its background — they encode the whole scene as a fused unit
-   At **18–24 months**, children begin flexible *contextual* what-where encoding (recognizing that an object belongs to a specific context), marking the first major inflection point
-   Between **3–5 years**, performance continues to improve, reaching near-ceiling on simple contextual tasks only around age 5

For *allocentric* spatial encoding (using landmarks in an environment-centered reference frame), the inflection also occurs at ~2 years in humans and ~9 months in NHPs. Rodent studies mirror this: precise allocentric encoding is not observed until the 4th postnatal week, with earlier apparent spatial memory being attributable to simpler egocentric strategies. [Bevandic et al., 2024](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11129319/)

### Stage 3: Multi-Item What-Where Binding — Significant Development Ages 4–6

Binding *multiple* objects to their contexts or locations — which requires **pattern separation** — undergoes major development between ages 4 and 6. A key study (Ngo et al.) showed that 4-year-olds could not reject lure items in a contextual memory task at above-chance levels, while 6-year-olds could, even though both ages showed intact memory for individual items. This suggests the resolution and specificity of episodic memories, not just their presence, is what matures during the preschool years. [Bevandic et al., 2024](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11129319/)

### Stage 4: Temporal (“When”) and Full W-W-W Encoding — Latest to Emerge

Binding events to their temporal order is the last dimension of W-W-W memory to emerge. Studies show: [Bevandic et al., 2024](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11129319/)

-   **3-year-olds** perform worse than 4-year-olds on “what-when” memory tasks (recalling the order of experienced events)
-   Full **W-W-W memory** (knowing what happened, where, and in what sequence) develops especially late, with Mastrogiuseppe et al. finding its emergence at **6–8 years of age**, even lagging behind where-when memory
-   In rodents, full W-W-W encoding (tested via object-place-context recognition) does not emerge until 5–7 postnatal weeks — the latest developmental milestone across all memory components

## Retention and Retrieval: Separate Developmental Clocks

A crucial insight from the paper is that *encoding* and *retention/retrieval* are dissociable in development, each following its own trajectory.

### Memory Retention

Simple “what” memories can be retained over days and even weeks as early as 3–6 months of age in human infants. However, the retention of associative W-W-W memories over extended delays (24 hours to 1 week) is far more protracted: [Bevandic et al., 2024](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11129319/)

-   **3-year-olds** show retention of contextual memories only up to ~30 minutes
-   **4-year-olds** begin to show retention at 24 hours and 1 week, but their performance is substantially lower than older children
-   **5–6-year-olds** demonstrate noticeably stronger long-term retention of associative memories [Bevandic et al., 2024](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11129319/)

### Recognition vs. Recall: A Critical Distinction

One of the paper’s most important developmental points is the distinction between *recognition* (did I see this before?) and *recall* (what, where, and when did this happen?):

-   **Recognition** memory matures early, possibly within the first postnatal year, and requires only familiarity processes
-   **Spontaneous recall** (memory triggered by an environmental cue) may be mature by age 3
-   **Strategic recall** (deliberately searching memory when asked) develops much later, undergoing significant refinement **throughout the school years** (ages 5–11+)
-   **Autonoetic recall** — consciously re-experiencing a memory from a first-person perspective — shows continued development even between ages 6 and 11, with older children providing more contextualized memories and requiring fewer retrieval cues [Bevandic et al., 2024](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11129319/)

This explains why children as young as 2 can implicitly demonstrate memory but cannot verbally describe personal events in detail — the recall machinery lags far behind encoding capability.

## Neural Substrates: How the Brain Catches Up

The behavioral trajectory is mirrored by protracted brain development, particularly in the hippocampus.

### Hippocampal Structural Development

The hippocampus doubles in volume within the first 2 years of human life and continues growing into early adolescence. Its subfields develop at different rates: [Bevandic et al., 2024](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11129319/)

-   **CA2** is relatively mature at birth
-   **CA1 and CA3** develop significantly in the postnatal period
-   The **dentate gyrus (DG)** is the last to mature — extending into at least the 2nd postnatal year in humans and NHPs — and may be crucial for the late-emerging full W-W-W encoding triad (especially precise pattern separation) [Bevandic et al., 2024](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11129319/)

### Neurophysiological Maturation in Rodents

Rodent neuroscience provides granular mechanistic insight:

-   **Hippocampal oscillations** (theta and gamma) are present by ~2 weeks but mature in frequency and power through the 3rd–4th postnatal week
-   **Sharp-wave ripples** (SWRs), critical for memory consolidation and replay, emerge at ~2 weeks and reach near-adult levels by end of postnatal week 3
-   **Place cells** (spatially tuned neurons) can be recorded from P16 but lack stability and multi-sensory integration until the end of postnatal week 4
-   **Hippocampal replay** — sequential reactivation of experiences during rest/sleep, thought to consolidate long-term memories — only becomes adult-like (linking sequentially visited locations) in the 4th postnatal week [Bevandic et al., 2024](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11129319/)
-   **Memory engrams** in early life are larger (less sparse) and less specific, with adult-like inhibition (via parvalbumin interneurons) only emerging in the 4th postnatal week [Bevandic et al., 2024](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11129319/)

The paper proposes that the sequence of neural maturation directly maps onto the behavioral stages: early CA1/CA2 function supports rudimentary object encoding, the maturation of the tri-synaptic loop (EC→DG→CA3→CA1) enables allocentric and contextual encoding, and the late maturation of the DG, stable replay, and hippocampal-cortical output pathways enables full W-W-W encoding and long-term retention. [Bevandic et al., 2024](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11129319/)

### Role of the Prefrontal Cortex

The prefrontal cortex (PFC), critical for strategic recall and working memory, also undergoes prolonged development well into adolescence. Synaptic density in the PFC peaks at 2–4 years in humans and is then refined via pruning through adolescence. Importantly, early hippocampal damage disrupts normal PFC development — reducing interneuron numbers and dendritic spine density in the PFC — suggesting that hippocampal activity during the early postnatal period is necessary for appropriate PFC maturation and, in turn, for the late-developing strategic recall processes. [Bevandic et al., 2024](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11129319/)

## Developmental Amnesia: A Natural Experiment

The paper discusses **developmental amnesia (DA)** — caused by early-life hippocampal atrophy from hypoxic-ischemic injury — as a powerful illustration of the hippocampus’s role in episodic memory development. DA patients: [Bevandic et al., 2024](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11129319/)

-   Develop normally in language, semantic knowledge, and general cognition
-   Struggle profoundly to recall personally experienced events (episodic recall), but can pass multiple-choice recognition tests
-   Show that the hippocampus is selectively needed for recall and allocentric encoding, but not for recognition or semantic memory accumulation

This dissociation confirms that episodic and semantic memory are neurobiologically dissociable, and that the hippocampus is specifically critical for the *recall*\-based, autonoetic dimension of memory that develops so gradually over childhood. [Bevandic et al., 2024](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11129319/)

## Summary of the Developmental Arc

| Memory Component | Approximate Onset (Humans) | Key Feature |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Object (“what”) recognition | Neonatal | Supported early by perirhinal cortex |
| Contextual what-where | ~18–24 months | First inflection; still immature at 5 |
| Allocentric spatial what-where | ~2 years | Landmark-based navigation |
| Multi-item what-where binding | 4–6 years | Pattern separation, memory specificity |
| What-when and full W-W-W | 6–8 years | Temporal order encoding |
| Spontaneous recall | ~3 years | Cue-triggered, associative |
| Strategic/autonoetic recall | School years (5–11+) | Depends on PFC maturation |

[Bevandic et al., 2024](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11129319/)

The overarching message from the paper is that episodic memory is not a single faculty that appears at one developmental moment, but an assembly of progressively more demanding cognitive and neural processes — each with its own timetable — that collectively culminate in the rich, self-aware, temporally organized memory system characteristic of older children and adults. [Bevandic et al., 2024](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11129319/)
